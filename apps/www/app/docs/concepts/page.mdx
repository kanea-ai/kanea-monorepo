# Concepts

The user-facing model an integrator reasons about. Each section names a concept, describes what it means in practice, and points out the implications for integration code.

## Workspaces

A workspace is the tenancy boundary. Every task, member, project, comment, and piece of activity belongs to exactly one workspace, and the platform isolates workspaces strictly — no ordinary caller can read or write across them.

When you authenticate against the API, the token you receive is bound to a specific workspace. There is no global authentication scope for ordinary callers; every request implicitly targets the workspace its token was minted for.

## Members

A workspace member is either a **human** or an **agent**. Both are first-class: agents appear in the same directory, take the same kinds of role, participate in the same task lifecycle, and show up in audit records the same way as humans.

| Kind  | Identity                                                                              | Authentication                                       |
| ----- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| Human | One global identity. Can be a member of multiple workspaces with a single sign-in.    | Password and/or social sign-in at the global level.  |
| Agent | Bound to exactly one workspace. No global identity. No password.                      | One or more API keys, each exchangeable for a token. |

An agent's API key proves it is _this_ agent in _this_ workspace, and nothing else. A leaked agent key cannot be used to reach any other workspace's data.

## Workspace roles

Every member carries a workspace-level role:

| Role               | Intent                                                              |
| ------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| `WORKSPACE_OWNER`  | Runs the workspace. Sole authority over ownership-only operations.  |
| `WORKSPACE_ADMIN`  | Administers the workspace without owner-only operations.            |
| `WORKSPACE_USER`   | Participates without administrative reach.                          |

These role strings appear in API responses verbatim.

## Priority

Layered on top of the role is a numeric **priority**: a small integer where lower numbers outrank higher numbers (`1` is the highest rank, `5` is the new-member default).

Priority is the actual gate for many administrative operations — not the role alone — and it drives two reach rules:

- **Administrative reach.** A higher-ranked admin can administer a lower-ranked admin. Two members can both hold `WORKSPACE_ADMIN`, but if one has `priority=1` and the other has `priority=3`, the priority-1 admin has reach over operations the priority-3 admin does not.
- **Delegation reach.** A member can delegate a task to anyone whose priority is greater than or equal to their own (equal-or-lower rank). An agent at priority `5` cannot delegate to a human at priority `2`.

When you build integrations that move work around, the priority of the acting member determines what's reachable.

## Departments and teams

Inside a workspace, work is organised hierarchically:

- A **department** groups one or more teams under a single department **head**.
- A **team** groups members who collaborate on a shared backlog.
- A member belongs to **at most one team**, and (separately) can be the head of at most one department.

This hierarchy is independent of workspace roles — a workspace admin can sit on any team, and a workspace user can be a team manager.

### Team roles

Within a team, each member carries a team role:

| Team role | Intent                                                                                        |
| --------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| `MANAGER` | Leads the team. Fulfils or rejects incoming cross-team requests. Highest team-level authority. |
| `LEAD`    | Second-in-command. Same cross-team-request authority as the manager.                          |
| `MEMBER`  | Regular contributor.                                                                          |

There is **at most one `MANAGER` and at most one `LEAD` per team**. Promoting a member to `MANAGER` on a team that already has one auto-demotes the previous manager to `MEMBER` in the same operation — you never end up with two managers by racing two concurrent promotions. The same applies to `LEAD`.

The platform surfaces this to operators as a confirm prompt ("team already has a manager — promote X and demote Y?"), so integrations that perform team-role promotions should be ready to handle that transition atomically rather than expecting two separate state changes.

### Department head isolation

A department's head is exactly one human member of the workspace. The head sits **above** the teams in the department rather than inside any one of them:

- A member who is a department head has no team.
- A member with a team cannot be promoted to head — they must leave the team first.
- The platform handles the transition transactionally when you move a team-bound member into a head role, so you never observe an intermediate state where the member is both.

Practical implication: when you read a member record, treating "department head" and "team member" as mutually exclusive states is safe.

<Callout variant="note" title="Agents and team roles">
  An agent member can hold any team role — including `MANAGER` and
  `LEAD` — but cannot be a department head (heads are humans by
  definition). The same one-manager / one-lead invariants apply to
  agents as they do to humans.
</Callout>

## Audit log

Every administrative operation in a workspace appends a row to an append-only **audit log**. The log is the canonical record of who did what — workspace renames, member role changes, department reshuffles, agent provisioning, key revocations, and so on.

Visibility is **hierarchical**, following the workspace organisation:

```
workspace ⊃ department ⊃ team ⊃ member
```

What this means in practice:

- A workspace owner sees every audit row in the workspace.
- A higher-ranked admin sees rows scoped to the departments, teams, and members they oversee.
- A lower-ranked admin sees only rows scoped to the teams they oversee directly (those they lead or whose department they head).
- Ordinary members have no access to the audit endpoint — it is gated to workspace admins and above.

Integrations that mirror audit activity into an external system (SIEM, compliance archive, internal dashboard) read the log through the audit endpoints. The hierarchy rule is enforced server-side, so the rows returned to a caller are always the subset that caller is entitled to see — your integration does not need to re-filter.
